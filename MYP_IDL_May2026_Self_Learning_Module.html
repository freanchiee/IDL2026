Identity formation is a consequence of the interactions between our genetics and our environment.

These are key terms from the PRM. Understanding them deeply will help you write at a Grade 7 level.
Nature
The genetic and biological influences on a person's traits, behaviour and health — inherited from parents via DNA.
Nurture
Environmental influences: family, education, culture, socioeconomic status, relationships, experiences.
Nominative determinism
The idea that people are drawn to life paths that match or reflect their name (e.g. a person named Baker becoming a pastry chef).
Somatotyping
A system classifying body types (ectomorph, mesomorph, endomorph) — once linked controversially to personality and athletic ability.
Heritability
A statistical measure (0–1) of how much genetic variation accounts for differences in a trait within a population. Used in twin studies.
Identity formation
The process by which individuals develop a sense of self — shaped by both biological (genetic) and environmental (social) factors.
Synthesis (IDL)
Using knowledge from TWO disciplines together to create a new, deeper understanding — the core skill assessed in this exam.
Socioeconomic status
A measure combining income, education and occupation that reflects social and economic position — a key environmental (nurture) factor.

GRADE 3 — basic understanding

The twin studies use science because they look at DNA and heritability. The nominative determinism video uses society because it talks about how names affect people's jobs. Both sources are about identity. The twin studies are stronger because they have real data. The nominative determinism video is weaker because it only uses examples.

Why it's Grade 3: Describes each source separately rather than comparing together. No explanation of HOW the disciplines are synthesised. Conclusion is simplistic with no evaluative depth.

GRADE 4 — basic interdisciplinary understanding with some critical thinking

Both the twin studies and the nominative determinism video use science and I&S to explore identity, but in different ways. The twin studies use scientific methods (heritability data showing 0.6+ correlations for personality traits) and connect to I&S by showing how genetic similarities affect social behaviours and relationships. The nominative determinism video uses social evidence (the Winner/Loser story) and connects to biology by implying names may have psychological-biological effects. The twin studies present a stronger synthesis because the scientific data is clearly linked to social outcomes — we can see how genetics shapes social identity. Nominative determinism is more speculative, relying on individual cases rather than statistical evidence, which weakens its scientific credibility even though the social argument is compelling.

Why it's Grade 4: Makes some comparison throughout. Uses evidence from both disciplines. Conclusion is more evaluative. Missing: doesn't go deep enough on WHY each synthesis approach has its particular strengths/weaknesses.

GRADE 7 — comprehensive synthesis with sophisticated critical thinking

Both the twin studies and the nominative determinism video attempt to synthesise sciences and individuals & societies to illuminate how identity forms, yet they do so through fundamentally different epistemological approaches that reflect the strengths and limitations of each discipline's methodology. The twin studies achieve their synthesis through quantitative scientific evidence — heritability scores demonstrate that identical twins raised apart share remarkable similarities in personality (heritability ~0.5) and health — which I&S uses to understand why social institutions, from healthcare systems to educational policies, should account for genetic variation. The synthesis's strength lies in its replicability and statistical robustness; its weakness is that reducing identity to heritability scores risks overlooking the dynamic interplay between genes and social environment that I&S scholars emphasise. Nominative determinism inverts this approach: starting from a social observation (historical name-occupation correlations), it borrows biological plausibility by suggesting psychological-neurological pathways that link nominal identity to behaviour — yet the scientific evidence is correlational at best, and the Winner/Loser case study, while compelling, represents an anecdote rather than data. This synthesis's strength is its accessibility and its ability to prompt critical reflection on how society constructs identity narratives; its weakness is its speculative scientific foundation, which a scientist would rightly challenge as lacking experimental rigour. In conclusion, the twin studies achieve a more robust interdisciplinary synthesis because quantitative data from science genuinely enriches the I&S understanding of how genetic variation shapes social inequality — while nominative determinism, though provocative and valuable for raising critical I&S questions about naming and power, rests on a weaker scientific foundation that limits its explanatory depth.

Why it's Grade 7: Directly compares throughout. Explains HOW disciplines synthesise (not just what they contain). Uses specific evidence. Evaluates both sources' methodological strengths/weaknesses. Reaches a nuanced, justified conclusion.

GRADE 4 — good understanding with critical thinking

I believe nurture has a greater influence on identity formation. The nominative determinism video provides social evidence: the Loser/Winner case study shows that despite having the same genetic background (they are brothers), their social environments led to completely different identities and outcomes — Loser became a police detective while Winner ended up in prison. This directly challenges the primacy of nature. Scientifically, from Reference 1, we know that environment shapes how genetic traits develop — even a naturally talented basketball player cannot reach their potential without training and coaching. However, the twin studies in Reference 3 present evidence against my position: heritability scores of 0.5–0.7 for personality traits suggest genetics plays a significant role. I would counter this by noting that heritability scores measure variance within populations, not the absolute contribution of genetics — in different environments, the same genotype can lead to very different outcomes. In conclusion, while both nature and nurture contribute to identity, the evidence from the PRM suggests environment has greater practical influence because it determines how genetic potential is actualised.

Why it's Grade 4: Uses both disciplines. Addresses counter-argument with some depth. Makes a nuanced point about heritability. Could be improved with a more sophisticated evaluative conclusion.

GRADE 7 — comprehensive, nuanced, fully justified

I argue that, while both nature and nurture indisputably shape identity, the evidence in the PRM demonstrates that nurture exerts a quantifiably greater practical influence on the formation of social identity — the version of self that we construct in relation to others and that society responds to. The most compelling scientific evidence comes from the nature and nurture video itself: even traits with a genetic basis (height, athletic capacity) require specific environmental triggers — coaching, nutrition, cultural expectations — to manifest as identity-defining characteristics. The social evidence reinforces this: the Winner/Loser case study is remarkable precisely because two individuals sharing near-identical genetic heritage diverged so dramatically. This cannot be explained by genetics alone; the social meaning attached to their names — a fundamentally environmental factor — appears to have shaped their self-concept and social opportunities, illustrating how I&S variables can override or redirect biological potential. Against my position, the twin studies demonstrate that heritability scores for personality dimensions reach 0.5–0.7, suggesting a substantial genetic contribution. I acknowledge this evidence, but I would counter that heritability statistics describe populations in specific social contexts — change the environment (different country, different socioeconomic circumstances), and the same genotype produces a different phenotype. The socioeconomic health data in Reference 5 corroborates this: individuals with identical genetic risk profiles for conditions like hypertension face dramatically different health outcomes based on access to resources, demonstrating environment's amplifying or buffering role. In conclusion, the preponderance of interdisciplinary evidence — from both the biological sciences and social analysis — supports nurture as the more powerful shaper of practical identity, because environment determines not only whether genetic potential is expressed, but also which expressions of identity are socially validated, rewarded, or suppressed.

Why it's Grade 7: Uses both disciplines throughout with specific evidence. Makes sophisticated scientific points (heritability in context). Addresses counter-argument with depth. Reaches a nuanced, well-justified conclusion that synthesises the disciplines.

GRADE 3 — stating challenges and solutions

The first challenge of somatotyping is that it puts people in boxes. This is bad because people are more than their body type. A solution is to teach people that body type does not determine who you are. The second challenge is that it could be used to discriminate. A solution is to make laws against discrimination.

Why it's Grade 3: Challenges and solutions are stated but not explained with depth. No use of PRM evidence. No interdisciplinary synthesis — doesn't connect science and I&S.

GRADE 4 — outlining with some creative thinking

Somatotyping presents two key challenges. First, scientifically, it oversimplifies the relationship between genetics and body characteristics. As the twin studies in Reference 3 show, even genetically identical individuals can develop differently based on nutrition and environment — meaning body type is not purely genetic and cannot reliably predict identity or success. Second, from an I&S perspective, classifying people by body type reinforces social stereotypes and can be used to exclude certain groups from opportunities (for example, assuming an endomorph is not athletic, limiting their sports access). A solution to the first challenge is to educate students about heritability — using twin study data to show that genetic expression is always mediated by environment. A solution to the second challenge could involve policy-level changes, such as requiring sports selection processes to be based on performance data rather than body type classification, preventing discriminatory practices. Both solutions require synthesising scientific literacy (understanding genetics) with social awareness (understanding discrimination).

Why it's Grade 4: Outlines challenges with some depth. Uses PRM evidence. Shows some interdisciplinary thinking. Solutions are practical. To reach Grade 7: needs deeper explanation and more sophisticated use of evidence.

GRADE 7 — explaining with comprehensive interdisciplinary synthesis

Somatotyping — the classification of individuals into ectomorph, mesomorph, and endomorph categories — presents challenges that are simultaneously scientific and social, requiring an interdisciplinary lens to fully understand and address. The first challenge is scientific: somatotyping creates an overly deterministic model of the relationship between genetics and phenotype that contradicts the more nuanced picture presented by the PRM. As the nature and nurture video makes clear, and as the twin studies confirm, even genetically identical individuals develop differently based on environmental inputs — nutrition, training, socioeconomic conditions. Presenting body type as a fixed, heritable predictor of success or identity ignores this complexity. The heritability data shows that personality and capability are partially but not entirely genetic, meaning any system that uses a single morphological classification to predict identity has fundamental scientific weaknesses. The solution here is interdisciplinary: using science education to make heritability concepts accessible, so that both individuals and institutions understand that genetic expression is always context-dependent. This would require I&S insights to identify which social groups are most vulnerable to deterministic thinking — for example, lower-income communities with less access to scientific literacy education. The second challenge is sociological: throughout history, body classification systems have been weaponised to justify social hierarchies and discrimination, and somatotyping carries this risk. As Reference 5 illustrates, social factors already produce profound inequalities in health and identity outcomes — layering a pseudoscientific body-type classification on top risks amplifying existing biases (e.g. assuming mesomorphs are naturally suited to leadership, excluding others). The solution requires structural I&S interventions — policy reforms that prohibit the use of somatotype-based classifications in hiring, sports selection, or health assessment — backed by the scientific evidence from twin studies demonstrating that performance and health are multi-factorial and environmentally mediated, not reducible to body type. Together, these solutions demonstrate that addressing the challenges of somatotyping requires neither science alone nor social analysis alone, but a genuine synthesis: the scientific case against determinism empowers the social case for equity.

Why it's Grade 7: Explains both challenges with depth and nuance. Uses multiple PRM sources as evidence. Solutions are sophisticated and truly interdisciplinary — each solution draws on BOTH sciences and I&S. Final sentence explicitly articulates the synthesis.
